# Representation and chart integrity

## Choose the form the claim earns

- Use prose for nuance, caveats, definitions, and claims without stable structure or comparable numbers.
- Use a diagram for process, hierarchy, causality, relationship, topology, or system behavior.
- Use a chart for comparable quantitative values with known units and defensible aggregation.
- Use an interaction when reader action exposes a meaningful comparison, sequence, layer, or consequence.
- Omit material that cannot support the story or its evidence contract.

Do not turn every number into a chart or every concept into a diagram. Representation is an editorial claim about what matters.

## Chart checks

Record and visibly preserve:

- measurement and unit;
- population, denominator, and time period;
- categories and ordering;
- baseline and axis domain;
- aggregation and transformation;
- missing values and exclusions;
- uncertainty or estimate status;
- source and as-of date.

Zero baselines are normally required for length encodings such as bars. If a nonzero baseline is analytically necessary, disclose it prominently. Do not use area or volume when the data is one-dimensional unless the encoding is explicitly normalized and explained.

## Illustrative data

Illustrative values may demonstrate a layout or interaction only when labeled in the visible artifact, the claim ledger, and the review record. Never let illustrative values migrate into sourced or publish-ready status.

## Platform reconstruction

When moving from web to carousel or platform output, preserve the claim set, source meaning, uncertainty, and causal order. Recompose density, rhythm, safe zones, and calls to action for the platform. Do not crop or resize the web artifact into panels.
