# An Argument in Ruins

Let the remains disagree. Build a lost civilization through material evidence, later reuse, and competing interpretations so players can investigate a people rather than receive a finished encyclopedia.

Anchor every invention in the user's canon and in the civilization's environment. Show how land, resources, neighbors, labor, belief, and technical practice shaped one another. Give the culture internal differences: authority and dissent, ideals and ordinary compromises, celebrated achievements and costs paid out of sight.

## Bury a history that can be recovered

- Establish what the civilization called itself, what later peoples call it, and what each name assumes.
- Trace a few consequential eras through visible changes in settlement, craft, governance, ritual, and contact with outsiders. Let chronology remain as precise as the evidence permits.
- Make decline multicausal. Separate what happened, what accelerated it, who benefited, and what remains genuinely unknown.
- Design a material signature—construction logic, repair habits, recurring motifs, tools, refuse, burial practice, or altered landscape—that allows discoveries to connect across sites.
- Preserve everyday life beside monuments. Food stores, children's marks, patched objects, and contested spaces often reveal more than royal inscriptions.
- Carry the legacy forward through descendants, stolen prestige, mistranslation, adaptive reuse, surviving practices, and present-day claims over the ruins.

## Return an archaeological mystery

Open with the civilization's central paradox. Give a compact historical reconstruction, then a field guide to sites and evidence. For each discovery, state what is directly observable, the leading interpretations, who advances each interpretation, and what further evidence could change the conclusion. Close with living stakeholders, player-facing leads, GM-only truths, and one question the current evidence cannot honestly resolve.

Keep the culture humanly plural and the evidence playable. Distinguish fact, inference, propaganda, and proposal; let discoveries revise the story.
