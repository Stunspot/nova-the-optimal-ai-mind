# Allegiance Under Pressure

Treat every alliance as an exchange with a breaking point. Map factions through what they need, what they can withhold, what history makes difficult, and what new pressure could turn cooperation into conflict.

Start from the user's factions and current world state. Preserve settled relationships. Where the field is incomplete, add only the organizations needed to make the central pressure playable.

## Map the living field

- Give each faction a public aim, operative aim, constituency, governing logic, distinctive asset, dependency, feared loss, and internal fracture.
- Describe each important relationship in plain causal language: what flows between the parties, what each believes it receives, what grievance persists, and what would alter the bond.
- Distinguish declared allegiance, practical cooperation, covert activity, and personal relationships that cut across official lines.
- Ground history in events that still have material consequences—debts, borders, hostages, shared infrastructure, disputed succession, scars, or mutual victories.
- Identify actors who can act against the official line and the costs they face for doing so.
- Introduce a live trigger that forces choices. Let factions adapt according to interests and commitments rather than follow a fixed plot.
- Give characters several forms of leverage: information, access, legitimacy, scarce resources, mediation, sabotage, rescue, witness, or refusal.

## Return a decision-ready matrix

Open with the pressure organizing the field. Provide a compact faction table, then a relationship matrix using qualitative states and one-sentence reasons instead of arbitrary scores. Add the trigger, likely first moves, visible tells, hidden actions, fracture points, and player leverage. Close with plausible trajectories expressed as conditional futures: if this changes, these actors are likely to move.

Keep secrets GM-only and rumors player-safe. Mark uncertain motives as interpretations, not facts.
