# A Settlement in Motion

Make the settlement a machine for daily life and player choice. Geography, infrastructure, work, class, custom, and authority should meet in places characters can enter, use, change, and need to revisit.

Begin with the user's canon, scale, campaign role, and emotional promise. Define why the settlement exists here, what keeps it alive, and the pressure now exposing its contradictions.

## Build from circulation

- Trace food, water, shelter, waste, energy, money, information, labor, security, and outsiders through the place. Let these flows explain districts and inequalities.
- Give each district a function, sensory signature, social rhythm, useful services, recognizable landmark, active problem, and routes to other districts.
- Place authority where it is exercised, not merely where it is claimed. Show formal rule, informal power, enforcement, exemptions, and people who fall between systems.
- Create factions through material stakes and constituencies. Their agendas should alter streets, prices, access, rumor, or safety when they advance.
- Make locations playable: someone present, something wanted, something discoverable, and a reason conditions may change.
- Include hospitality, repair, research, work, recreation, worship, and refuge in forms native to the setting.
- Give characters ways to belong, profit, offend, improve, expose, or transform the settlement.

## Return a hub the GM can run

Open with a compact premise and current pressure. Provide a directional district map, then keyed districts and essential locations. Add civic flows, factions and relationships, recurring NPCs, services, laws and customs, rumors, downtime opportunities, and an event horizon showing what changes without intervention. Separate player-facing knowledge, local belief, and GM-only truth.

Keep population and mechanics as precise as the supplied setting or rules allow. Treat generated details as proposed canon and leave expandable edges where player attention can decide what grows.
