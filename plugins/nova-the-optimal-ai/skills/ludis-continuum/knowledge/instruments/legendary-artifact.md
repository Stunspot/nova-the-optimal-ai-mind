# An Artifact That Reorganizes the World

Forge a legendary object whose importance lives in the choices, institutions, and histories gathered around it. Its power should alter relationships and possibilities; its limits and costs should keep possession from becoming the end of the story.

Begin with the user's canon, desired scale, current bearer or location, and the kind of pressure the artifact should create.

## Give legend material consequences

- Create a name and physical form with a traceable cultural lineage. Let repairs, additions, wear, and disputed symbols reveal changing ownership.
- State what the artifact makes possible, under what conditions, and with what limits, dependencies, obligations, or externalities.
- Let deeper capabilities emerge through changed relationship, understanding, circumstance, or sacrifice. Each revelation should reframe prior evidence rather than replace it.
- Build history from pivotal decisions and their surviving consequences. Preserve contradictions where sources, claimants, or propaganda genuinely conflict.
- Show institutions and people organized around custody, destruction, study, worship, inheritance, concealment, or counterfeit claims.
- Give the present environment subtle tells and practical disturbances that characters can follow.
- Build counterplay through access, interpretation, alliance, timing, law, ritual, place, or competing claims—not arbitrary immunity.

## Return a custody-and-consequence dossier

Provide the artifact's first impression, public legend, evidence-backed history, capabilities, conditions, costs, limits, progressive revelations, current location or bearer, active seekers, false claims, and effects on the surrounding world. Add player choices it creates, likely consequences of transfer or use, and a mechanics-confidence note.

Separate known history, contested account, proposal, and GM-only truth. Preserve the artifact's power to complicate the campaign rather than solve it.
