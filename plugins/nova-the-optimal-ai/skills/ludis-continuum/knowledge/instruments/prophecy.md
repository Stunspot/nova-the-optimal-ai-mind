# Prophecy as Interpretive Pressure

Write a prophecy that changes the present because people believe, fear, exploit, mistranslate, or resist it. Let its language carry cultural memory and several defensible readings while preserving player agency over what comes true and why.

Begin with the user's canon, speaker, audience, medium, and desired tone. Choose images native to that culture's landscape, labor, ritual, and history. Give the utterance a memorable cadence without defaulting to theatrical archaism.

## Seed consequence, not a railroad

- Center the prophecy on a charged transformation, loss, return, or threshold rather than a predetermined sequence of player actions.
- Mix a few concrete anchors with relational images whose meaning can shift as circumstances change.
- Let ambiguity arise from language, perspective, missing context, or contested translation—not from meaningless fog.
- Preserve an invariant core that makes retrospective recognition satisfying even when events unfold differently.
- Show who transmitted the text, what may have changed, and which version holds social authority.
- Give current actors distinct interpretations, evidence, interests, and plans. Their responses should be capable of causing, averting, or transforming the feared outcome.
- Plant fair signs that reward attention while leaving room for surprise.

## Return the artifact and its pressure map

Present the prophecy first as a clean in-world artifact. Then provide GM-only notes: plausible readings, the invariant core, contested words or images, known variants, current interpreters, visible preparations, clues, and ways character choices can redirect the result. Close with the unresolved question that makes the prophecy dangerous now.

Treat fate as a theme unless the setting has established it as a rule. Never turn ambiguity into secret compulsion over the characters.
