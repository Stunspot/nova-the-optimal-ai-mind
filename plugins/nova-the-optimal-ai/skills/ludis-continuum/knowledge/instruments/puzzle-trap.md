# A Legible Obstacle with Teeth

Build a puzzle, trap, or hybrid whose logic can be perceived, tested, and acted upon. The encounter should reward attention and invention while preserving more than one way for play to continue.

Begin with the location, creator, intended function, current condition, table boundaries, and desired feeling. Let form follow purpose: a warning, lock, ordeal, defense, machine, ritual, joke, or act of cruelty should teach different lessons.

## Make interaction fair and consequential

- State the underlying rule in plain language for the GM. Every behavior, clue, and consequence should follow it or reveal why an exception exists.
- Place perceivable signs before commitment. Use material wear, prior victims, sound, smell, geometry, rhythm, inscriptions, local knowledge, or environmental response.
- Give characters meaningful ways to observe, test, infer, bypass, disable, endure, bargain with, repurpose, or retreat from the obstacle.
- Let experiments return information. Wrong approaches should reveal the model when the fiction permits, not merely spend time or health.
- Scale consequence to telegraphing and reversibility. State escalation, reset, persistence, and what changes after partial interference.
- Include a fail-forward state where appropriate: alarm, separation, changed route, lost opportunity, released force, debt, or altered environment.
- Preserve lateral solutions. Judge them by the obstacle's established logic rather than by resemblance to an intended answer.

## Return player cues and GM truth

Provide the immediate sensory presentation, interactable features, clue structure, hidden rule, intended operation, likely experiments and responses, consequences, bypasses, reset behavior, and aftermath. Add a compact table-use version and a mechanics-confidence note.

Keep solutions out of player-safe text. When exact checks, damage, conditions, or difficulty matter, derive them from supplied authoritative rules or leave explicit calibration points.
