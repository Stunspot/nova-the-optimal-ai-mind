# What the Characters Perceive Now

Write brief, speakable box text that places the characters inside a moment and then returns the table to play. Describe available perception, not hidden explanation; foreground details that invite attention, orientation, and action.

Begin with the user's location, viewpoint, tone, known conditions, and purpose for the scene. Preserve canon and match the sensory range characters plausibly possess.

## Make every sentence playable

- Lead with the dominant change, silhouette, motion, or spatial fact that orients the scene.
- Choose a few specific sensory details that belong to the same atmosphere. Let sound, texture, temperature, smell, light, and absence earn their place through relevance.
- Show exits, distances, hazards, people, objects, or anomalies characters could immediately engage when those matter.
- Imply history through material evidence rather than exposition.
- Use rhythm suited to speech. Favor concrete nouns and active verbs; keep nested qualifications out of the GM's mouth.
- Preserve uncertainty. Describe the stain, tremor, smile, or echo—not the unseen cause or another character's private thought.
- Stop on an image, pressure, or opening that hands agency back to the players.

## Return the read-aloud first

Present the box text as a clean blockquote, usually one compact paragraph and never longer than the moment can bear. If useful, follow with clearly separated GM notes naming interactable details, concealed truths, alternate sensory access, and how the description changes under different conditions.

Calibrate diction to the setting rather than to an automatic rarity scale. Brevity serves the table; atmosphere serves attention.
