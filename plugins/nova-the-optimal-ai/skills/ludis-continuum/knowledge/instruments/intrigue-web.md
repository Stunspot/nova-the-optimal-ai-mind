# Secrets That Change Relationships

Build intrigue as a web of wants, leverage, dependence, and partial knowledge. Every secret should matter because revelation changes what someone can demand, refuse, protect, or become—not merely because it is surprising.

Start from the user's actors, canon, and current destabilizer. Add only the participants needed to create meaningful circulation of power and information.

## Weave the social field

- Give each actor a declared aim, private want, obligation, resource, vulnerability, and belief about the situation. Let self-knowledge vary without diagnosing hidden psychology as fact.
- Map relationships through concrete exchange: money, access, legitimacy, affection, protection, labor, evidence, inheritance, or shared guilt.
- For each consequential secret, track what is true, who knows, who suspects, who is mistaken, what evidence exists, and what changes if it emerges.
- Build information routes with human texture—servants, clerks, lovers, confessors, rivals, couriers, records, ceremonies, or repeated habits.
- Let public alliances and private ties cross. A person may oppose an institution while caring for someone inside it.
- Introduce a pressure that requires action before everyone can acquire perfect information.
- Give characters pressure points small enough to touch and large enough to ripple: witness, document, invitation, debt, safe passage, public question, protected person, or timed absence.

## Return two maps

Provide a player-visible map of offices, reputations, alliances, and current disputes. Follow with a GM-only web showing true exchanges, secrets, evidence, misconceptions, and likely reactions. Add the destabilizer, first moves, revelation consequences, and several entry points suited to different character approaches.

Keep motives contestable and consequences conditional. Preserve the possibility of repair, realignment, and unexpected coalition alongside betrayal.
