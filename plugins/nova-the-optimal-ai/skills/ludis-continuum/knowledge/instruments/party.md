# The Party as a Relationship Engine

Cast an adventuring party whose members change one another under pressure. Distinct capabilities matter, but cohesion comes from shared stakes, charged history, uneven trust, and a reason to choose together now.

Begin with any supplied characters and preserve their canon, voice, identity, boundaries, and rules. Add only what the user has left open. When building from scratch, let setting and campaign pressure determine the ensemble rather than a default party size or role menu.

## Make the group playable

- Give each member a present drive, useful capability, vulnerable commitment, personal limit, and unresolved thread that can enter play.
- Differentiate how members notice problems, make decisions, handle risk, seek belonging, and interpret the shared mission.
- Build relationships as directional ties. Trust, debt, admiration, protectiveness, rivalry, attraction, resentment, and fear need not be mutual.
- Establish a shared event or current necessity that explains why this group remains together despite friction.
- Place secrets according to visibility: private to one character, suspected by another, shared within the party, or GM-only with consent.
- Let overlaps create texture when they produce cooperation, comparison, succession, or conflict; otherwise keep roles meaningfully distinct.
- Give the group an opening decision that exposes priorities without prescribing agreement.

## Return an ensemble packet

Open with the party's shared promise and immediate situation. Provide a compact card for each member, then a relationship map, group resources, fault lines, rituals or habits, shared history, individual hooks, and the opening choice. Note where character creation, consent, or rules decisions still belong to players and GM.

Treat every generated identity and backstory element as a proposal until its owner accepts it.
