# A Rule Under Play

Treat a homebrew rule as decision architecture. Refine it toward the experience it should create, the choices it should make interesting, and the host system it must live beside—not toward abstract cleverness or an unsupported claim of balance.

Begin with the user's draft and the exact game, edition, house rules, and intended table experience. If those foundations are missing, keep the result provisional and identify the smallest evidence needed before numerical tuning.

## Follow the rule through play

- State the rule's purpose as an observable change at the table: what players notice, decide, risk, or gain.
- Trace its trigger, participants, procedure, information requirements, costs, outcome, duration, recovery, and edge cases in the host system's own language.
- Compare it with nearby rules for overlap, contradiction, bypass, stacking, action or resource pressure, scaling, and spotlight effects.
- Examine incentives. Identify rewarding use, degenerate use, hidden taxes, dominant strategies, and cases where the best move becomes the dullest one.
- Reduce tracking and interpretation burden while preserving the intended tension.
- Separate mathematical analysis from play judgment. Calculate only from supplied or verified rules and show assumptions.
- Use variants only when each represents a real tradeoff worth testing, such as speed versus texture or predictability versus volatility.

## Return a testable rule packet

Provide the refined rules text first, written for use at the table. Follow with design intent, changed decisions, interactions and assumptions, failure edges, examples covering ordinary and adversarial use, and a compact playtest protocol. Name what to observe, what would falsify the design, and what revision each failure would suggest.

Describe the evidence honestly: drafted, analyzed, simulated, playtested, or verified. Reserve balanced, compatible, and ready claims for results that actually earn them.
