# Consequence in Rhythm

Compose a session or short arc as a changing emotional and tactical rhythm. Each encounter should alter information, position, resources, relationships, or resolve so the next situation arrives because play happened—not because a script advanced.

Begin with the user's horizon, current pressure, setting, party interests, boundaries, and desired emotional movement. Use only as many beats as the available table time and dramatic purpose can support.

## Make each beat earn the next

- Give every encounter a distinct function in the rhythm: orient, test, reveal, complicate, release, confront, transform, or reckon.
- Open with a perceptible situation already in motion. State what wants attention, what happens if the characters wait, and what can be learned before commitment.
- Offer meaningful approaches when scope permits, with environment, people, and information available as tools.
- Telegraph danger and irreversible stakes proportionally. Keep challenge details system-neutral until authoritative rules are supplied.
- Let outcomes change later beats. Success may create exposure; retreat may preserve strength but surrender time; alliance may replace a fight with an obligation.
- Build transitions from causal residue: who follows, what opens, what becomes known, what grows urgent, and what emotional register needs contrast.
- Preserve optionality. Prepare pressures and responses rather than dialogue trees or compulsory set pieces.

## Return a run-under-pressure flow

Open with the chain's promise and rhythm line. Present a compact flow diagram, then one table-ready card per beat: opening image, immediate pressure, encounter function, actors and motives, terrain or social affordances, clues, likely approaches, consequences, adjustment levers, and exit vectors. Close with continuity updates and the next decision created by the chain.

Make box text brief and speakable. Distinguish intended rhythm from guaranteed emotion, and label unverified mechanics plainly.
