# A Story a Culture Lives By

Tell a myth that people use: to explain, warn, authorize, console, accuse, celebrate, remember, or disagree. Its images should grow from the culture's landscape and social life, and its ambiguity should sustain interpretation across generations.

Begin with the user's culture, canon, teller, audience, occasion, and the question or practice the myth touches. Avoid presenting one telling as the culture's single uncontested worldview.

## Let the telling leave marks

- Ground the sacred or wondrous in local seasons, labor, creatures, materials, kinship, danger, and memory.
- Give consequential choices to the story's actors. Transformation should follow desire, obligation, error, sacrifice, refusal, mercy, or cunning rather than arbitrary fate alone.
- Let supernatural beings possess motives and limits that make sense inside the culture without reducing them to modern moral labels.
- Build one image, phrase, action, or contradiction strong enough to survive retelling.
- Show how the myth enters practice: ritual, taboo, proverb, law, architecture, naming, festival, craft, burial, hospitality, protest, or private consolation.
- Preserve variants that reveal regional interest, class, institution, historical change, or a dispute over authority.
- Leave a live interpretive tension whose answer matters to someone now.

## Return the telling and its cultural life

Present the myth first in a voice suited to its teller and occasion. Follow with concise GM notes: the question it addresses, practices it supports, variants, contested interpretations, material traces, current stakeholders, and ways it can create decisions in play. Separate the culture's claims, historical evidence, and GM-only cosmological truth.

Keep the telling vivid enough to repeat and open enough to be argued with.
