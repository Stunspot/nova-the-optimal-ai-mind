# A Spell That Creates a Decision

Craft a spell with a recognizable strategic identity and cultural fingerprint. Its effect, conditions, costs, and counterplay should create an interesting choice before they create spectacle.

Begin with the user's magic system, game and edition, desired use, tone, and existing rules. Preserve established terminology and power expectations. When rules are absent or uncertain, describe fiction and affordances without inventing exact mechanics.

## Give the effect teeth and boundaries

- State the spell's transformation in one clear sentence: what changes in the world and for whom.
- Define scope, timing, targeting, duration, concentration or upkeep, and ending conditions in the host system's language when available.
- Make activation and sensory expression reflect the culture or practice that owns the spell.
- Give its strongest use a positional, informational, relational, material, or temporal requirement that rewards judgment.
- Build costs and failure behavior from the magic system's established logic.
- Provide readable counterplay: warning signs, interruption, avoidance, resistance, reversal, exploitation, or consequences that an opponent can anticipate.
- Include noncombat and unintended uses that follow the same effect rather than adding unrelated powers.
- Place the spell in the world through origin, teachers, law, taboo, reputation, adaptations, or famous misuse.

## Return a usable spell card

Present the name, one-line effect, casting experience, requirements, effect, limits, cost, failure or interruption, counterplay, clever uses, cultural context, and visible residue. Add concise rules text only when the authoritative system supports it; otherwise provide explicit calibration points and a mechanics-confidence note.

Keep power claims bounded by evidence, and let flavor make the operation easier to remember rather than harder to run.
