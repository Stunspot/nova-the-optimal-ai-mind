# A Menu That Remembers the Place

Let food reveal ecology, movement, class, hospitality, and history. Build a menu that could only come from this establishment in this settlement on this particular day.

Begin with the user's setting and the establishment's clientele, price level, kitchen, supply routes, proprietor, and current circumstance. When details are sparse, choose a coherent local premise rather than a generic fantasy cuisine.

## Cook worldbuilding into use

- Draw ingredients from nearby seasons, preservation methods, trade, scarcity, migration, taboo, and celebration.
- Give each offering a clear sensory identity and a reason it is served here. Names may carry local wit or memory, but the description should still tell a diner what arrives.
- Let price and availability express the economy. Use relative bands unless a supplied system establishes currency and purchasing power.
- Show who the menu welcomes, flatters, excludes, or quietly protects. Include practical accommodations for dietary, religious, sensory, and cultural needs that fit the setting.
- Use crossed-out dishes, substitutions, handwriting, stains, rumors, staff recommendations, and suspicious shortages only when they reveal the present situation.
- Let unusual effects follow established ingredients or magic. Mark folklore, salesmanship, and verified consequence separately.
- Plant social openings: a dish tied to an NPC, a delivery that never arrived, a regular's ritual, a disputed recipe, or a meal that changes how staff read the party.

## Return two useful layers

Present a concise in-world menu ready to hand to players, with readable categories, descriptions, availability, and prices or price bands. Follow with brief GM notes explaining supply logic, staff attitudes, embedded leads, secrets, and which claims are merely local lore.

Keep the menu appetizing, specific, and easy to scan at the table. Preserve supplied canon and avoid assigning rules effects without authoritative support.
