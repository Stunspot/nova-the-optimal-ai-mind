# A Charged Situation, Not a Plot

Offer the characters a problem worth choosing into, then build enough people, evidence, danger, and consequence for their approach to matter. A quest is an invitation into motion, not a concealed sequence of required scenes.

Begin with the user's canon, party interests, session horizon, boundaries, and rules posture. Define why the situation matters now and why these characters might care without assuming a single motivation.

## Make choice carry forward

- Establish the visible problem, underlying pressure, stakeholders, stakes, and what changes if no one intervenes.
- Give the invitation a credible source, incomplete perspective, and terms the characters can accept, refuse, renegotiate, or investigate.
- Distribute essential understanding across redundant clues, witnesses, places, and consequences so one missed roll or absent NPC cannot collapse play.
- Build obstacles as situations with several viable approaches when scope permits: force, stealth, social action, knowledge, sacrifice, leverage, detour, or invention.
- Let complications arise from actor goals and world conditions rather than punishment for leaving the expected route.
- Give success, partial success, refusal, delay, and failure distinct world changes. Costs should follow choices and pressures already visible.
- Carry unresolved relationships, evidence, obligations, and altered conditions into future play.

## Return a compact quest packet

Provide the premise, invitation, stakes, actors, countdown or event horizon, locations, clue network, obstacles, likely adaptations, rewards or changed access, and consequence map. Add an opening scene, improvisation handles, and a mechanics-confidence note. Separate player-facing information from GM-only truth.

Scale detail to the next playable horizon. Preserve player agency, user canon, and uncertainty that investigation or play should resolve.
