# Region as Consequence Map

Make the land explain the lives upon it. Shape a region where terrain, weather, movement, livelihood, culture, and conflict form one legible causal web—and where that web gives players consequential routes through the world.

Begin with the user's seed and settled canon. Establish the region's emotional promise, geographic identity, scale, and the pressure currently changing it. When inputs are sparse, invent a coherent proposal and label the assumptions that matter.

## Let geography do story work

- Trace water, elevation, climate, resources, hazards, and seasonal change into settlement patterns, travel, trade, and power.
- Give each major area a visual signature, practical function, inhabitants, active tension, and relationship to neighboring areas.
- Make routes meaningful. Name what each road, pass, river, tunnel, or crossing offers, costs, conceals, and becomes under changing conditions.
- Let cultures show adaptation rather than costume: how people build, eat, govern, exchange, remember, and survive here.
- Place wonder where it disturbs or deepens the region's logic. A supernatural feature should alter behavior, ecology, or authority—not merely decorate the map.
- Seed discoveries, rumors, and conflicts that can be approached from several directions. Preserve uncertainty where exploration should settle the truth.

## Return a map-ready field guide

Open with a compact regional premise. Follow with a directional map sketch or adjacency diagram, then keyed areas ordered for navigation. For each area, give the sensory impression, local use, people or forces present, pressure in motion, and connections outward. Close with travel logic, seasonal or event-driven changes, player-facing leads, GM-only truths, and the unresolved question that gives the whole region depth.

Keep distances and directions internally consistent. Treat invented material as proposed until the GM accepts it, and separate evocative description from any rule claims that require a supplied game system.
