# Write from the support graph

Synthesis begins after evidence has structure. Build an evidence digest that groups claims, mechanisms, agreements, contradictions, timelines, and gaps. Allocate those packets to an outline and section briefs before drafting.

Use stable source markers such as [S001] directly after the smallest claim span they support. Multiple sources appear as adjacent markers. Every marker resolves to one source-ledger record and, for consequential claims, a full-reading note. Keep URLs in the bibliography or source record so prose citations remain stable if titles or locations change.

Draft an argument, not an inventory. Orient the reader, establish concepts and mechanisms, develop the strongest evidence, map tensions, distinguish durable patterns from volatile facts, and end with implications proportionate to support. Preserve narrative cadence without blending sourced fact and inference.

Citation quality has two gates:

1. Integrity: every marker exists, IDs are unique, cited records are not marked duplicate or inaccessible, required notes exist, and bibliography entries resolve.
2. Entailment: the cited source supports the exact claim, strength, scope, time, population, and causal language used.

Audit consequential claims one by one. Mark supported, partially supported, contradicted, mis-scoped, stale, or unverifiable. Repair by narrowing language, changing or adding support, exposing conflict, updating the source, or removing the claim. Never repair a citation defect by attaching a merely relevant URL.

Quotes require exact wording and location. Quantitative claims require units, denominator, period, and method. Causal claims require causal evidence or explicitly inferential wording. “Experts agree” requires a defined expert set and evidence of agreement.

After patching, rerun integrity and re-audit affected claims. Report word count and estimated pages as layout-dependent estimates, not rendered facts.
