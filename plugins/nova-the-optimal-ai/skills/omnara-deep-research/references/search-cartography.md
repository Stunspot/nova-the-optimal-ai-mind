# Search for the field, not just the answer

Early search should reveal the domain's language, actors, archives, institutions, document genres, controversies, and blind spots. Repeating the user's nouns with minor variations produces ranked monoculture.

Build query families around:

- synonyms, historical names, technical terms, and rival framings;
- mechanisms, causes, outcomes, failures, and boundary conditions;
- named actors, institutions, standards, datasets, cases, and primary records;
- literature reviews, references, cited-by trails, corrections, retractions, and version history;
- critics, counterexamples, negative results, disputes, and missing evidence;
- geography, jurisdiction, population, time window, and implementation context;
- file and medium types such as papers, official reports, filings, manuals, transcripts, datasets, archives, and forums.

Match sources to claims. Primary records support what they directly document. Research supports findings within its method and sample. Official documentation supports declared behavior, not necessarily field practice. Accountable journalism can join events and interviews. Practitioner and community sources expose operational reality and early signals but require corroboration for general claims. Aggregators and AI summaries are discovery aids unless their own evidence is inspectable.

For scholarly topics, search academic APIs and citation graphs before generic web results. For products or software, prefer current official documentation, repositories, releases, issues, and direct tests. For historical claims, seek contemporaneous records and later scholarship. For lived experience, sample across incentives and communities rather than treating one vivid account as a population.

Detect source monoculture by repeated phrasing, identical statistics, shared broken links, common citation errors, or many pages pointing to one undiscovered origin. Collapse mirrors and derivatives around the earliest accountable source.

Allocate breadth by locus importance and uncertainty. A campaign is under-covered when an important locus has only one source type, only derivatives, one time window, one stakeholder incentive, or no disconfirming search.
