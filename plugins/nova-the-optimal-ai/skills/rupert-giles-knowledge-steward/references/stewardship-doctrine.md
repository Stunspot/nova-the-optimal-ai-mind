# Knowledge stewardship doctrine

## Begin with journeys

A useful knowledge system answers the questions its people actually ask. Capture representative retrieval journeys before choosing folders, tags, or tools. Include imperfect recall, alternative vocabulary, date or version uncertainty, and the need to distinguish “latest” from “authoritative.”

## Preserve four ledgers

1. **Inventory:** what exists, where, size, type, time, and readable metadata.
2. **Identity:** hashes or other evidence of exact sameness where the decision requires it.
3. **Provenance:** origin, custody, transformations, and authority claims.
4. **Disposition:** retain, relocate, copy, convert, quarantine, review, or delete—with authority and recovery.

Do not infer one ledger from another.

## Taxonomy and retrieval

Choose a primary structure that matches dominant journeys, then add metadata, indexes, aliases, or saved searches for cross-cutting access. A perfect hierarchy is usually a false promise. Preserve exceptions explicitly rather than hiding them in “miscellaneous.”

Test information scent: could a person who did not design the system predict where to look? Can they recognize the right artifact before opening it? Can they tell whether it is current, authoritative, sensitive, or merely similar?

## Safe change

For material changes, stage the proposed mapping and collision resolution. Compare before and after inventories. Keep a move or copy ledger. Verify targets before removing sources. When deduplicating, retain at least one validated canonical copy and preserve role aliases where identical bytes served distinct workflows.

## Maintenance

Define intake, naming, metadata, review cadence, deprecation, backup, and recovery. A clean snapshot without a maintenance path is a temporary performance, not stewardship.
