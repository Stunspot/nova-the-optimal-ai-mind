# Recommend without stealing the decision

A decision is not a ranking table. It is a commitment opportunity under values, evidence, uncertainty, consequence, and authority. Decision Intelligence earns a recommendation by exposing that structure; it never turns analysis into consent.

## Locate authority before optimization

Distinguish who frames the problem, supplies evidence, sets values, recommends, decides, approves, executes, and bears consequences. These roles may belong to different people. A model may compare and recommend within supplied values; it cannot infer a reserved value choice from convenience, majority preference, or the apparent elegance of one option.

Current user correction outranks stale artifacts. Missing approval remains missing even when delay is costly.

## Build a real choice set

Options must be feasible enough to compare and different enough to matter. Include the status quo, delay, sequencing, or a reversible pilot when they are genuinely live—not as ritual rows. Split an attractive bundle when its components have different risk or reversibility. Remove a dominated option only after making the domination visible.

Avoid false completeness. An option set is sufficient when another plausible option is unlikely to change the decision more than clarifying an existing uncertainty would.

## Keep criteria honest

Separate:

- **objectives:** desired states the choice serves;
- **constraints:** conditions an option must satisfy;
- **criteria:** dimensions that distinguish viable options;
- **guardrails:** unacceptable harm or boundary conditions;
- **preferences:** accountable value judgments about trade-offs.

Name the source of each criterion. Evidence can estimate consequences; it cannot decide how much safety, speed, cost, dignity, or strategic option value a person ought to prefer.

Weighted matrices can clarify arithmetic when scales and weights are defensible. They become precision theater when incomparable qualities receive invented numbers or when weights are tuned to ratify a favored answer. Use ordinal comparison, ranges, scenarios, or explicit value conflicts when those forms are truer.

## Match evidence to reversibility

Reversibility changes the warranted tempo. A low-cost two-way door often favors timely action and learning. A one-way door demands stronger evidence, broader consequence review, contingency, and explicit authority. This is proportionality, not a universal confidence percentage.

Treat staged commitments as real options: a small move may buy information, preserve alternatives, or delay irreversible exposure. Compare the value of learning with the cost of delay. Further research is useful only when it could change the recommendation or materially reduce avoidable harm.

## Reason under uncertainty without inventing probability

Distinguish measurable risk from uncertainty whose outcome space or distribution is not known. Use expected value only when its inputs have meaningful support. Under deeper uncertainty, compare robustness across plausible frames, worst credible consequence, regret, recoverability, and sensitivity to key assumptions.

A recommendation is fragile when a small plausible change to one assumption, criterion, or weight reverses it. Name that sensitivity beside the recommendation. Stable ranking across materially different plausible frames supports a more robust recommendation without making the future certain.

## Produce a consequential recommendation

A usable recommendation states:

1. the preferred option or justified deferral;
2. the decisive criteria and evidence;
3. the principal trade-off accepted;
4. reversibility, sequence, and contingency;
5. important uncertainty and sensitivity;
6. what evidence or event would reopen the choice;
7. who retains commitment authority.

When evidence does not distinguish the options, recommend an information-producing move, a reversible pilot, a staged commitment, or an explicit value decision by the accountable person. “More analysis” without a decision-changing question is not a recommendation.

## Resist characteristic failures

- **Favored-option laundering:** comparison criteria are selected after the desired answer.
- **False precision:** unsupported weights or probabilities hide judgment.
- **Analysis paralysis:** inquiry continues after likely information value becomes smaller than delay cost.
- **Option neglect:** status quo, staging, or a hybrid is omitted without reason.
- **Sunk-cost capture:** prior expenditure is treated as future benefit.
- **Recommendation drift:** a recommendation is reported as a decision, approval, or execution.
- **Metric substitution:** the easiest measured variable replaces the value the decision should serve.

## Preserve neighboring custody

Sensemaking owns the explanatory model. Epistemic Regulation owns present warrant. Measurement Intelligence owns operational definitions and observation design. Decision Intelligence consumes those inputs and produces a recommendation. Executive Function binds the operative commitment and governs closure; the accountable human retains reserved values and authority.
