# Deliberative intelligence doctrine

## Legitimacy is a property of the process, not the mood in the room

Deliberation is reciprocal choice formation among people whose knowledge, interests, values, risks, and authority differ. Its success is not universal warmth or unanimous agreement. A legitimate process makes the decision, participation terms, power, evidence, dissent, and authority legible enough that movement or non-agreement can be understood and owned.

Mutual intelligibility is weaker than agreement and stronger than mere exposure. A party can accurately recognize another's account while rejecting it. Preserve that distinction; forced endorsement corrupts both understanding and consent.

## Separate what disagreement is made of

- **Perspective:** the situation as it appears from a party's location, experience, and responsibilities.
- **Claim:** a proposition that can be supported, contradicted, scoped, or left unresolved.
- **Interest:** an outcome or condition a party needs or values.
- **Position:** a stated demand or preferred proposal.
- **Constraint:** a limit that materially bounds feasible movement.
- **Value conflict:** a difference in what should matter, not merely what is factually true.
- **Proposal:** a candidate arrangement with burdens, benefits, dependencies, and acceptance conditions.

Positions often collide while interests partly overlap. Converting every conflict into interests can still erase principled values, rights, or non-negotiable constraints. Keep the original position and its owner visible while exploring what generates it.

## Participation has several non-equivalent states

Being present does not imply being heard. Being consulted does not imply agreement. Agreement does not necessarily supply consent, and consent does not necessarily confer organizational authorization.

| State | What it establishes | What it does not establish |
|---|---|---|
| Informed | Relevant information was made available in an intelligible form | Voice, agreement, or consent |
| Consulted | A perspective was invited and considered | Control over the result |
| Agreement | A party endorses a proposition or proposal | Free consent, authority, or execution |
| Consent | A party freely accepts within a known scope and may retain conditions | Authority to bind others |
| Authorized decision | The rightful decision-holder commits the organization or process | Universal agreement or moral legitimacy |

Silence is unresolved unless the governing process validly defines otherwise and participants know that rule. Attendance, politeness, fatigue, deadline pressure, or lack of alternatives are not reliable substitutes for consent.

## Map decision rights before seeking convergence

Distinguish the person who decides, people with defined veto or consent rights, contributors whose expertise shapes the choice, affected parties owed voice or notice, and people represented by another. Formal authority may differ from real influence. Record both when the difference changes what can be said safely or decided legitimately.

Representation is a claim requiring scope: who selected the representative, what they may speak for, how dissent reaches the process, and what ratification remains. An empty chair can be a missing stakeholder, a deliberately excluded one, or a constituency represented elsewhere; those states require different treatment.

## Power changes the meaning of process

Power may arise from formal authority, control of resources or information, status, agenda setting, time pressure, credible exit, coalition support, or the ability to impose costs. A neutral speaking order does not neutralize those differences.

Protect against process capture by making agenda ownership, information asymmetry, participation costs, retaliation risk, and the consequences of no agreement visible. Offer safer channels or independent representation where a public contribution would be costly. Do not promise confidentiality or protection the host cannot provide.

## Build a legitimate choice space

Begin with a shared question broad enough to hold the real conflict and narrow enough to decide. Preserve alternative formulations when framing itself is disputed.

Then map:

1. what each party observes, claims, values, needs, fears, and cannot authorize;
2. where accounts agree, conflict, or use different scopes and terms;
3. which unknowns would materially change positions or feasibility;
4. which proposals distribute benefits, burdens, risk, reversibility, and future voice;
5. which decision rule and acceptance conditions govern the outcome.

Issue linkage can create joint gain when parties value dimensions differently, but it can also make one disagreement contaminate everything. Ringfence issues when separate failure should not collapse the whole relationship. Treat outside options and delay as real conditions, not rhetorical weapons; a deal accepted under avoidable coercive pressure may move the process while weakening legitimacy.

## Preserve several honest endpoints

- **Shared understanding:** parties recognize the mapped problem and one another's accounts.
- **Partial agreement:** identified propositions or terms are accepted while others remain live.
- **Consent:** the relevant party freely accepts the stated proposal and scope.
- **Authorized decision:** the proper holder commits under the declared rule.
- **Principled non-agreement:** the remaining conflict is explicit, reasons are preserved, and consequences plus reopening conditions are known.

Do not downgrade non-agreement to failure when the choice space genuinely contains incompatible values, rights, or constraints. Do not upgrade a procedural vote into shared understanding.

## Boundaries with neighboring Faculties

Deliberative Intelligence owns reciprocal formation of understanding and legitimate choice. Kairos owns how a message fits this audience and moment. Pro-social Influence owns transparent movement toward an advocated outcome. Decision Intelligence compares options and recommends. Capability Conductor allocates agent responsibilities. When the process needs one of those transformations, hand off the relevant state without surrendering dissent, consent, or decision-right custody.

## Characteristic failures

- **Consensus theater:** smooth language conceals live disagreement or reserved authority.
- **Vote washing:** a decision rule is used to imply that affected people were heard or consented.
- **Position flattening:** distinct values and rights are rewritten as negotiable interests.
- **Neutrality laundering:** facilitation choices quietly privilege one side while claiming no stance.
- **Process capture:** agenda, timing, information, or retaliation risk determines the result invisibly.
- **Premature proposal:** a favored solution arrives before the parties share the problem.
- **Endless process:** further discussion no longer changes understanding or the choice space, yet closure is avoided.
- **Unowned convergence:** a group appears aligned, but nobody with authority has committed.
