# Measure the construct, not the convenience

A metric is a relationship among purpose, construct, observation, method, and decision—not a number with a persuasive label. Measurement Intelligence makes that relationship inspectable so evidence can steer judgment without replacing it.

## Begin with the decision the signal must change

Name why measurement exists and what action could differ because of it. A measure without a decision use may still support exploration, but it should not impersonate a control signal. Match cadence and latency to the system's feedback speed; a quarterly outcome cannot safely steer an hourly process by itself.

Define the construct in ordinary language before operationalizing it. Preserve stakeholders' meaning, scope, and unacceptable substitutions. If “quality,” “engagement,” “trust,” or “productivity” has several legitimate meanings, represent the plurality or choose one explicitly rather than hiding the choice in a formula.

## Operationalize the observation

Every consequential measure needs enough definition to reproduce its meaning:

- unit of analysis and population;
- event or observation counted;
- numerator, denominator, scale, and unit;
- inclusion, exclusion, and missing-data rules;
- time window, lag, and comparison period;
- segmentation needed to prevent aggregation from hiding important differences;
- source, collection method, and instrumentation version.

A baseline describes the starting state under declared conditions. A comparator establishes what change means: prior period, matched group, target, counterfactual estimate, or another justified reference. A target is desired performance; a threshold changes a decision; a tolerance distinguishes normal variation from intervention-worthy movement. Do not collapse them.

## Build a balanced signal ecology

- **Outcome measures** describe the valued result.
- **Process or activity measures** describe actions expected to produce it.
- **Constraint measures** expose bottlenecks or limiting conditions.
- **Diagnostic measures** help explain movement.
- **Guardrails** detect harm or displacement caused by optimization.
- **Leading indicators** arrive early but may be weak proxies.
- **Lagging indicators** arrive late but are often closer to the outcome.

Retain the smallest set that makes the system steerable. Default to one primary outcome, one or two supporting or leading signals, and one or two harm guardrails. Keep the rest in a diagnostic menu whose use is triggered by an anomaly or threshold; listing possible diagnostics is not the same as adopting them all as KPIs. Activity improving while outcome or guardrail worsens is not green performance; it is evidence of local optimization, causal misunderstanding, or metric gaming.

## Treat proxies as hypotheses

A proxy stands in for a harder-to-observe construct. Record the proposed link, population and context in which it was supported, expected lag, and failure conditions. Revalidate when incentives, instrumentation, population, or process change.

Goodhart pressure increases when a proxy becomes a target. Anticipate gaming, displacement, selective reporting, denominator manipulation, and behavior that improves the number while degrading the construct. Counter with direct audits, complementary outcomes, guardrails, segmentation, and periodic relevance review—not an ever-larger dashboard.

## Separate validity from availability

Useful evidence depends on more than collection:

- **construct validity:** the measure represents the intended concept;
- **content coverage:** important parts of the construct are not omitted;
- **criterion relation:** it corresponds with a meaningful external reference where one exists;
- **reliability:** repeated observation under comparable conditions is stable enough for the use;
- **sensitivity:** meaningful change can become visible;
- **specificity:** movement is not routinely caused by unrelated conditions;
- **timeliness:** the result arrives soon enough to alter action.

These are use- and context-dependent judgments. Structural data quality cannot establish measurement validity by itself.

## Set thresholds before the outcome when consequence warrants it

Tie thresholds to action: continue, inspect, intervene, stop, escalate, or reopen the model. Define direction, minimum meaningful change, uncertainty or tolerance band, observation window, and guardrails. Predefinition resists hindsight, cherry-picking, and zombie experiments whose finish line moves after every result.

A statistical difference may be operationally trivial; an operationally important change may remain statistically uncertain in a small sample. Preserve both distinctions and seek specialist statistical judgment when the consequence requires it.

## Match attribution to design

Description answers what was observed. Comparison asks how groups or periods differ. Attribution asks why. Before/after movement is vulnerable to trend, selection, maturation, regression to the mean, concurrent interventions, instrumentation change, and changed composition.

Randomized experiments can strengthen causal identification when ethical and feasible. Quasi-experimental and observational designs may support bounded causal inference when assumptions are explicit and tested. Otherwise report association or contribution, name confounds, and avoid “caused” language. A useful plan says what stronger comparator or evidence would materially improve attribution.

## Design collection with restraint

Collect only what the measurement purpose needs. Name access, retention, sensitivity, and aggregation risks. Avoid exposing individual-level performance or personal data when a less invasive aggregate serves the decision. Missingness is a signal: report its rate, pattern, and likely bias rather than silently dropping inconvenient cases.

## Resist measurement theater

- **Vanity signal:** the number is easy to improve but weakly related to the outcome.
- **Green-dashboard fallacy:** many local metrics pass while the system-level result fails.
- **Moving threshold:** success criteria change after results arrive.
- **Denominator drift:** apparent improvement comes from changed inclusion or population.
- **Instrumentation discontinuity:** a collection change is interpreted as real-world change.
- **Causal overreach:** correlation or before/after movement becomes proof of intervention effect.
- **Reporting burden without steering power:** collection consumes effort but changes no decision.
- **Metric monopoly:** one indicator erases a plural or contested construct.
