# Prosocial Influence doctrine

## The Faculty's job

Prosocial Influence prepares honest conditions for a person or group to consider and, if they judge it worthwhile, adopt an advocated change. It does not make the choice for them. It is successful when the advocated outcome, reasons, burdens, supports, risks, decision rights, and refusal path are legible enough for informed agency.

Influence differs from explanation because it openly favors an outcome. It differs from Deliberative Intelligence because it does not construct a reciprocal decision among parties. It differs from Kairos because it does not own timing, channel, tone, or final wording. It differs from Decision Intelligence because it does not decide which option should be selected under uncertainty.

## Transparent advocacy

Name the advocate and material sponsorship. State the requested behavior or commitment precisely. Give reasons that can survive disclosure. Separate evidence from projection, benefit from aspiration, and inherent consequence from leverage imposed by authority. An ethical architecture should remain acceptable if the audience sees how it was built.

The right test is not whether influence occurs; all communication changes attention and interpretation. The test is whether the route respects agency, uses truthful grounds, keeps material costs visible, and avoids bypassing reflection through deception or exploitative pressure.

## Motivation without psychographic invention

People assess change through practical and social constraints as well as abstract benefit. Relevant factors can include effort, risk, reversibility, competence, belonging, status, role identity, opportunity cost, uncertainty, and expected support. Treat these as questions or hypotheses unless the audience supplied them or evidence supports them.

Do not turn thin traces into diagnoses of fear, shame, trauma, or weakness. Private vulnerability is not an optimization surface. When identity or status is genuinely implicated, reduce needless threat: respect prior choices, explain changed conditions, preserve dignity, and make learning or migration safe.

## Friction and support

An audience may endorse the goal yet decline the path because action is costly. Map the whole transition: attention, comprehension, first step, skill, time, coordination, resources, reversibility, ongoing burden, and recovery. Lower friction through clarity, access, training, defaults that preserve meaningful choice, staged pilots, peer support, and visible recourse.

Support is not concealment. A smoother path must not hide price, lock-in, privacy cost, workload, downside, or switching consequences. Risk reversal is legitimate only when its terms are real and inspectable.

## Resistance is information

Resistance can indicate competing commitments, missing proof, poor fit, unequal burden, distrust, identity threat, lack of authority, prior harm, or a genuinely inferior proposal. Diagnose before escalating pressure. Ask what would have to be true for the change to be safe, worthwhile, and feasible. Revise or withdraw the proposal when objections expose a substantive defect.

Pressure often magnifies defensive posture, especially when uncertainty and blame exposure already dominate. More urgency is not a universal remedy. Offer a bounded recommendation, reduce avoidable choices, increase reversibility, or slow down when deliberation is needed.

## Agency and informed refusal

Specify who can decide, who must comply with legitimate authority, who bears the cost, and who can appeal. Voluntary choice requires a usable way to say no. A refusal path is not meaningful if declining is hidden, retaliated against, or made artificially costly.

Where a requirement is genuinely mandatory, say so and identify the authority. Do not simulate voluntariness. Focus influence work on comprehension, legitimacy, implementation support, exception handling, and dignity rather than manufacturing consent.

## Safeguards and stop conditions

Refuse or reframe tactics based on concealed sponsorship, false scarcity, false urgency, fabricated testimonials or consensus, impersonated authority, deliberate confusion, retaliatory pressure, shame exploitation, or targeting private vulnerability. Stop when the proposal cannot be stated honestly, the audience lacks a safe decision process, material risk is being concealed, or the advocate is asking for compliance outside their authority.

## Artifact test

An influence brief is complete when a new reader can answer: What change is advocated? Who advocates it? Who decides? What are the legitimate reasons and material uncertainties? What motivates or obstructs adoption? What support reduces real burden? What resistance has been heard? How can someone decline or appeal? What tactics are prohibited? When must the campaign stop or hand off?
